# The Rebel, by Jean-Michel Dumay

- **Source**: https://archive.wikiwix.com/cache/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.lemonde.fr%2Fidees%2Farticle%2F2008%2F07%2F21%2Fl-homme-revolte-par-jean-michel-dumay_1075543_3232.html
- **Original Publication**: Le Monde, 21 July 2008
- **Translation**: English (Translated from the French original)

After exploring the foundations of the absurd (*The Stranger*, *The Myth of Sisyphus*, *The Misunderstanding*), Albert Camus (1913-1960) plunged as early as 1943 into the mass of documents that would constitute the material of his second "cycle", its counterpart, its consequence: revolt, whose movement is entirely driven by the absurd. Camus, nourished by his experience in the Resistance, devoted himself to it with a single soul. Four years after *The Plague*, he completed *The Rebel* in 1951, which he would consider the most important stone in his work.
As soon as the book was released, criticism fueled the waves. The essay was either praised or stigmatized. No indifference. Friends or enemies, or adversaries among his friends. He alienated Breton and the surrealists, Sartre and the existentialists. *Le Figaro littéraire* read it as a great text of the contemporary era, *Le Monde* praised the "moving essay of history and morals". "More than the awareness of an era by a lucid and courageous mind, we will soon see in it a reflection of the era upon itself," wrote Maurice Nadeau in *Combat*.

What then are this reflection, this turning point, the ferment of disorder? A condemnation of revolutions and absolute ideologies which, after having killed God and glorified history under the guise of liberating the community of men, lead to the bloody impasse of repression and murderous violence. In the middle of the Cold War, in the shadow of Stalin, the scope was significant. The subject electrified the left, breaking friendships.

To seal this condemnation, Albert Camus opens a long march through the history of revolt. Its originality? Not to separate the metaphysical revolt of man against his condition ("I rebel, therefore we exist") from the string of historical revolts that the centuries count, notably the 19th and 20th, where "the excess of the times" would fail. To arm his argument, the author summons the figures of Cain, Sade, Saint-Just, Lautréamont, Rimbaud, Bakounine, Nietzsche, and the Russian terrorists of the Narodnaya Volya (People's Will).

Along the way, it is less the causes of the revolt that he autopsies than its odious metamorphosis from a movement of liberation into a force of oppression. He revisites 1789 and its procession of scaffolds. And 1905, before 1917, when the nihilism of young irreducibles ended in terrorism. "Until them, men died in the name of what they knew or what they believed they knew. From them on, the more difficult habit was formed of sacrificing oneself for something of which they knew nothing, except that it was necessary to die for it to exist."

For Camus, a lucid step back forces us to consider that it is an essentially human, deep cause that generated such catastrophes. An infection that was gangrening the Western consciousness. And he identifies it. The culprit is nihilism—far beyond social, political, or economic history—present for several decades among poets, philosophers, and ideologues. This dark force nourishes the will to absolute power of totalitarianisms and perfumes abstract trials. This is called, on the one hand, fascism, which establishes on the altar of irrationality the reign of a handful of individuals and the enslavement of all others—but in 1951, that was already almost ancient history. It is also called Marxism, which, in the name of reason, to liberate the man of the future, enslaves him to the present.

"HE IS A MAN WHO SAYS NO"

Camus, the left-wing anti-communist, then proposes, not without lyricism, a "third way," which he wishes to be enlightened, that of measure—"neither victims nor executioners"—which rehabilitates revolt expurged of nihilism: "What is a rebel? He is a man who says no. But if he refuses, he does not renounce: he is also a man who says yes, from his first movement."

If the political circumstances have changed—the fall of the Wall obliges—it is not certain that the reflections aroused by *The Rebel* are outdated. Seized by the terrorism of September 11, the question of the use of violence is still relevant today. More than half a century ago, Camus targeted revolutionary violence, while Sartre wanted, for his part, to settle scores with the violence structurally imposed by social systems based on inequality (support the revolution and you were labeled an enemy of Liberty, defend Liberty and you were considered to be rejecting the only project challenging capitalism). Today, the suicidal violence of Al-Qaeda would resonate on one side, and the domination of a conquering Uncle Sam on the other...

With Ronald Aronson, American specialist on Sartre (University of Detroit), we can note how much of the first reflections on the war in Iraq "were based on models dating from the Cold War, and that sometimes this brought people back to Camus or Sartre" (*Cités*, January 2005). But beyond timely recuperation, one might also believe that *The Rebel*, having bathed in that bygone era of promises of singing tomorrows, has yellowed, that it has become useless. With Denis Charbit (Tel Aviv University, in *The Rebel, fifty years later*, Minard, 2001), we will nevertheless estimate that this is not the case: "It will remain for the rebels of today and tomorrow what Camus wanted it to be: reasons to believe in the dignity of their solitary momentum." These reasons must lean on a necessary duty of memory. And perhaps discard any hope in action from now on. Revolt—like the spirit of resistance—would now live in unison with the contemporary world. Without cherishing the future, which is uncertain, but in the present moment.
